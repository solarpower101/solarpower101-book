## Homeowner Scenario

You are considering a battery but need to know whether it solves a real problem or only makes the project more expensive.

## Required Inputs

- Outage frequency and typical outage duration.
- Critical loads such as refrigerator, medical equipment, internet, lights, or HVAC.
- Desired backup duration.
- Battery model, usable capacity, and power limits.
- Utility rate plan, export rules, and budget.
- Backup panel or load management requirements.

## Decision Process

1. List critical loads before discussing battery size.
2. Separate whole-home backup expectations from essential-load backup.
3. Compare battery usable capacity with desired backup duration.
4. Check whether rate-plan savings can justify any part of the cost.
5. Decide whether the battery is worth it, maybe, or probably not for your use case.

## Example Assessment

A homeowner who wants refrigerator, internet, lights, and outlets through short outages may be a good fit for essential-load backup. A homeowner expecting central air conditioning for multi-day outages needs a deeper design conversation and may face major cost increases.

## Red Flags

- Whole-home backup is promised without a load discussion.
- Battery capacity is quoted without usable capacity or power limits.
- Backup panel work is not included.
- Savings claims depend on utility rules that are not verified.

## Questions To Ask Installer

- Which loads will this battery actually support?
- How long would my priority loads run under typical use?
- Is a backup panel, load controller, or electrical upgrade included?
- How do utility rates affect the battery value?

## Decision Output

Create a battery fit assessment with priority loads, expected limits, required electrical scope, utility assumptions, and worth it / maybe / probably not guidance.
