Use these FAQs when solar becomes a real home project. Do not ask only, "Is solar good?" Ask, "What must be true for solar to make sense for this roof, bill, utility, financing offer, and installer?"

## Decision FAQs

### How do I know whether I am ready to request quotes?

You are ready when each installer gets the same facts: one recent electric bill, 12 months of usage if available, roof age, main panel photos, utility meter photos, shade concerns, and your main goal.

Without those inputs, treat early numbers as rough estimates. A quote should get tighter after the installer reviews your bill, roof, panel, utility rules, and site conditions.

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Practical test:</strong>
  <p>Would two installers use the same facts? If not, collect the missing facts before comparing savings claims.</p>
</div>

### What is the biggest mistake homeowners make when comparing solar quotes?

Comparing monthly payments instead of the full design. A lower payment can come from a smaller system, longer loan, higher financed price, dealer fees, different equipment, missing electrical work, or inflated savings.

Compare quotes in this order:

1. System size, panel count, inverter type, and roof layout.
2. Estimated annual production and the assumptions behind it.
3. Utility rate, export credit, fixed charges, and bill data used.
4. Cash price, financed price, APR, term, fees, and incentive assumptions.
5. Workmanship warranty, equipment warranties, exclusions, and service responsibility.

### Should I wait if my roof may need replacement soon?

Often, yes. Solar equipment can last for decades. Removing and reinstalling panels for roof work costs money. If the roof is near replacement age, get a roof opinion before signing.

Waiting does not mean rejecting solar. It can mean replacing the roof first, adding roof work to the project, using another roof plane, or delaying until roof timing is clear.

### Is a solar quote reliable before a site survey?

A pre-survey quote is not final. It can rely on satellite images, estimated roof measurements, assumed panel capacity, and rough shade modeling. A site survey can change the design because of roof condition, attic access, panel capacity, meter location, code rules, setbacks, or shade.

Ask what can change after the site survey and how changes affect price, production, financing, and cancellation rights.

### What should I do if an installer says the offer expires today?

Slow down. Solar is a construction, electrical, utility, and finance decision. A same-day deadline does not replace a written proposal, contract review, equipment list, utility assumptions, and cancellation terms.

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Practical response:</strong>
  <p>"I will review the full proposal, contract, financing terms, equipment list, production assumptions, and cancellation language before deciding. If the price depends on signing before I can review those, I am not ready to proceed."</p>
</div>

## Savings And Bill FAQs

### Why can two installers estimate very different savings for the same home?

Savings estimates depend on inputs. Two quotes can use different annual usage, production estimates, utility rate increases, export credit value, incentives, degradation, financing terms, and remaining bill charges.

Do not pick the higher estimate. Pick the estimate with better proof.

### Will solar eliminate my electric bill?

Usually no. Solar can reduce energy charges, but bills often keep fixed charges, minimum bills, taxes, fees, demand charges, or grid charges. Utility rules set export credits.

Ask which charges remain and what export credit was used.

### Should my system cover 100% of my usage?

Not always. The best size depends on roof space, shade, export rules, rate plan, future usage, budget, and the value of extra production. In some areas, a smaller system beats a 100% offset system.

Ask why the proposed offset is the right target for your rate plan and household plans.

### How should I think about future changes like an EV or heat pump?

Tell installers before final design. Past bills show old usage, not future usage. EV charging, heat pumps, induction cooking, pool equipment, household changes, and work-from-home schedules can change demand.

A good proposal says whether it sizes for current usage, future usage, or a phased plan.

## Battery FAQs

### Do I need a battery?

Only if it solves a real problem. Batteries can help with outages, time-of-use rates, low export credits, or energy independence. They also add cost, equipment, permits, placement rules, monitoring, and service needs.

If your main goal is bill savings and outages are rare, ask for solar-only and solar-plus-battery economics separately.

### Why does "whole-home backup" need careful review?

A home can draw more power than a battery can supply, especially with central air, electric heat, ovens, dryers, pumps, or EV charging. Start with critical loads: refrigerator, medical equipment, internet, selected outlets, lights, and limited heating or cooling.

Ask which circuits are backed up, what is excluded, expected runtime, and whether solar can recharge the battery during an outage.

### What does "battery ready" actually mean?

It can mean only that a battery can be added later. It does not mean the quote includes a battery, backup wiring, critical-loads panel, permits, or low future cost.

Ask what equipment, wiring, space, permits, and additional cost would be required to add a battery later.

## Incentive And Financing FAQs

### Should I compare gross price or net price?

Start with gross price. Net price can hide tax credit assumptions, rebates, lender rules, or incentives that arrive later. Compare full project cost first. Then list each incentive, timing, eligibility, and paperwork owner.

### Is the federal tax credit the same as a discount?

No. A tax credit estimate is not an instant installer discount. Value and timing depend on your tax situation and current rules. Confirm tax claims with a qualified tax professional.

### Why can financed solar cost much more than the cash price?

Financing can include interest, long terms, dealer fees, origination costs, and incentive-payment rules. A low monthly payment does not mean low total cost.

Ask for the cash price, financed price, APR, term, fees, payment schedule, lien or security-interest terms, and what happens if you do not apply an incentive to the loan.

### Is leasing or a power purchase agreement bad?

Not always, but it is different from ownership. A lease or power purchase agreement can reduce upfront cost, but it affects incentives, home sale transfer, payments, escalators, service, buyout options, and equipment control.

Compare ownership, lease, and power purchase options by total obligation, not just first-year monthly cost.

## Installer And Contract FAQs

### How many quotes should I get?

Get enough to see the range of designs and inputs. For many homeowners, two or three serious quotes work. More quotes do not help if they use different inputs or you compare only headline price.

Use the same bill, roof details, goal, and question list for each installer.

### How do I tell whether an installer is being transparent?

A transparent installer explains the proposal without jargon. They show equipment models, roof layout, production inputs, bill inputs, utility rules, exclusions, warranties, installer responsibility, and what can change after the site survey.

They also give you time to review the contract and financing documents before signing.

### What contract terms deserve extra attention?

Check payment milestones, cancellation rights, change orders, equipment substitutions, production estimate language, roof and workmanship warranty terms, lien releases, financing terms, dispute process, and changes from permits, utility approval, or site conditions.

If the sales conversation included a promise, make sure the contract or proposal says it clearly.

### What should I do if the final design changes after signing?

Get the change in writing before approving it. The update should state what changed, why, and how it affects price, production, equipment, timeline, financing, and cancellation options.

Do not assume a smaller system, different equipment, or lower production estimate is automatically equivalent to the original proposal.

## After Installation FAQs

### Why might the system be installed but not turned on?

Most grid-connected systems need inspection and utility permission before normal operation. Installation day is not the finish line. The project can still need inspection, utility paperwork, meter work, monitoring setup, or commissioning.

Ask the installer for the expected steps from installation through permission to operate.

### What records should I keep after the project is complete?

Keep the contract, final design, as-built documents, permit approvals, inspection approvals, permission-to-operate notice, equipment model and serial numbers, warranty documents, monitoring login, and installer support contacts.

These records help with troubleshooting, warranty claims, roof work, insurance, battery additions, and home sale.

### How do I know whether low production is a problem?

Check production against season, weather, shade, system age, monitoring status, and inverter alerts. A cloudy winter week is different from an inverter offline for a month.

If production drops suddenly, monitoring stops reporting, or an error appears, take screenshots and contact the responsible support channel.

## A Better Final Question

Instead of asking only "Is this a good solar deal?", ask:

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Can I explain why this system size, equipment, price, financing, utility input, incentive input, and installer fit my home?</strong>
  <p>If not, ask for the missing explanation in writing.</p>
</div>
