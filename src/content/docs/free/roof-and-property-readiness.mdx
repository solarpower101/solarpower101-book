## What You Will Learn

- Know which property details affect system design.
- Spot roof or electrical issues that may change scope.
- Prepare photos before a site review.
- Decide whether to request quotes now, fix the home first, or ask for a site visit before trusting numbers.

## Real-World Scenarios

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Scenario 1: the roof is 18 years old and the quote assumes a 25-year system.</strong>
  <p>Solar can still work, but signing before checking roof life can create removal and reinstallation costs later. Decide whether roof work comes before solar, belongs in the project scope, or waits.</p>
</div>

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Scenario 2: the satellite image looks clear, but afternoon shade crosses the west roof.</strong>
  <p>A quote based only on aerial images can overstate production. Ask for shade inputs, panel placement, and limits from trees, vents, chimneys, skylights, or nearby buildings.</p>
</div>

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Scenario 3: the main electric panel is full or hard to access.</strong>
  <p>The system may need a panel upgrade, relocation work, a different interconnection method, or extra permitting. This can change cost and schedule after the sales quote unless it is checked early.</p>
</div>

## Key Ideas

### Roof Condition Matters Before Solar Design

If a roof is near replacement age, it is often better to resolve that before installing equipment that may need to be removed for later roof work.

### Shade Changes Production Throughout the Year

Trees, chimneys, vents, and neighboring structures can reduce output at different times of day and in different seasons.

### Electrical Readiness Can Affect Cost

Main panel capacity, meter location, conduit runs, and local code requirements can change the installation scope beyond the panels themselves.

## Practical Steps

1. Estimate your roof age and note any repairs planned in the next few years.
2. Look for shade from trees, chimneys, neighboring buildings, or vents during the day.
3. Confirm who can approve changes to the property before scheduling a site review.
4. Take photos of the roof from the ground, attic access if available, the main service panel, utility meter, and likely equipment wall.
5. Write down any constraints: HOA rules, leased land, shared roof, limited driveway access, pets, locked gates, or planned remodeling.

## Questions To Ask

- Does my roof need repair or replacement before installation?
- Will vents, skylights, dormers, or trees limit the array layout?
- Do you expect a main panel upgrade or other electrical work?
- What photos or measurements do you need before you treat the quote as reliable?
- What project costs could change after the site survey?
- If the final roof layout changes, how will price, production estimate, and contract terms change?

## What Good Looks Like

<div class="homeowner-checklist">

## Watch-Outs

- Skipping roof condition questions because the solar quote looks attractive.
- Using only a satellite image when shade or roof complexity needs closer review.
- Forgetting HOA, landlord, or co-owner approval requirements.
- Assuming a low quote already includes roof repair, panel upgrades, or unusual access work.
- Signing before the site survey if the home has obvious roof, shade, or electrical uncertainty.

## Next Action

Take clear photos of the roof area, main panel, utility meter, likely equipment wall, and visible shading. Put them in one folder so every installer starts from the same facts.
