Solar sales often start with a monthly payment or claimed savings. Use a better path: learn what the system does, check the home, collect the right inputs, then compare quotes with the same assumptions.

## What You Will Learn

- Recognize the main parts of a rooftop solar system.
- Understand why production, usage, and utility rules all affect savings.
- Separate educational planning from quote-specific promises.
- Decide what to do next before requesting or accepting a quote.

Do not try to become an engineer overnight. Make the quote explain itself: what is being installed, why that size was chosen, what bill data was used, what utility rules were used, and what the headline number excludes.

## Key Ideas

### Solar Panels Produce Direct Current Electricity

Panels turn sunlight into DC power. An inverter converts that power into AC electricity that the home can use, and the utility meter records the exchange with the grid.

### System Size Is Only One Part of the Story

A larger system can produce more energy, but roof orientation, shade, equipment limits, local rules, and household usage determine whether that size makes sense.

### Savings Estimates Are Assumptions

A good quote states the inputs behind production, utility rates, incentives, and future bill changes. It does not treat projections as guarantees.

### Solar Is Not Automatically Backup Power

Most grid-tied systems shut down during an outage unless they include backup equipment. If backup matters, discuss the battery and backed-up circuits from the start.

## Practical Steps

1. Write one sentence that defines the job solar needs to do for your home.
2. Gather one recent electric bill and, if available, 12 months of kWh usage.
3. Take clear photos of the main electrical panel, utility meter, roof surfaces, attic access, and visible shade sources.
4. Estimate the roof age and note any roof repairs planned in the next five years.
5. List planned changes that may affect electricity use, such as an EV, heat pump, pool equipment, work-from-home schedule, or household size change.
6. Ask each installer to explain the design, assumptions, exclusions, and next steps in writing.

## Questions To Ask

- Which equipment will be installed, and where will it sit on the property?
- What annual production estimate are you using, and what assumptions drive it?
- How will the system interact with my current utility rate plan?
- What charges on my bill are likely to remain after solar?
- What changes if my roof, electrical panel, utility rules, or incentive eligibility differs from the quote assumptions?
- What written documents should I receive before I am expected to sign?

## What Good Looks Like

<div class="homeowner-checklist">
  <label><input type="checkbox" /> The quote shows system size, panel count, inverter type, estimated annual production, and roof layout.</label>
  <label><input type="checkbox" /> The savings estimate names the utility rate plan, export credit assumption, and bill data used.</label>
  <label><input type="checkbox" /> The proposal separates cash price, financed price, incentives, fees, and any optional battery cost.</label>
  <label><input type="checkbox" /> The installer can explain what happens during design review, permitting, installation, inspection, and permission to operate.</label>
</div>

## Watch-Outs

- Treating the monthly payment as the full project economics.
- Comparing quotes without checking system size, equipment, and production assumptions.
- Assuming solar automatically provides backup power without a battery or backup-capable setup.
- Letting a deadline or discount pressure replace a careful quote review.
- Comparing one installer's net-after-incentives price against another installer's gross price.

## Next Action

Collect one recent bill, write your main solar goal, and take photos of the roof area, main panel, and utility meter.
