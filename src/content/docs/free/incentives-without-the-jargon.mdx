## What You Will Learn

- Separate incentive categories from project pricing.
- Know which incentive claims need independent confirmation.
- Ask for quotes that show gross cost and incentives.
- Understand how incentive timing and eligibility can change the household cash flow.

## Real-World Scenarios

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Scenario 1: the quote subtracts a tax credit from the price.</strong>
  <p>You may need to pay or finance the full project cost first. A tax credit estimate is not a guaranteed signing discount. Its value depends on your tax situation.</p>
</div>

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Scenario 2: a rebate exists, but funds are limited.</strong>
  <p>Some rebates require pre-approval, specific equipment, income qualification, utility paperwork, or available funds. Ask who files, when approval happens, and what happens if the rebate is unavailable.</p>
</div>

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Scenario 3: two quotes show different "net" prices.</strong>
  <p>One installer may subtract every possible incentive while another shows only project cost. Compare gross price first, then compare each incentive assumption separately.</p>
</div>

## Key Ideas

### Incentives Can Come From Several Places

Federal, state, local, utility, and manufacturer programs may each have different eligibility rules, paperwork, deadlines, and funding limits.

### Estimated Incentives Are Not the Same as Cash in Hand

Some incentives reduce tax liability, some are rebates, and some are credits. The timing and actual value can vary by household and program rules.

### Good Quotes Show Before-and-After Pricing

Ask installers to show the project price before incentives, each estimated incentive separately, and the net figure after assumptions are applied.

## Practical Steps

1. List your state, utility, and city so incentive research starts in the right place.
2. Ask installers to separate equipment pricing from any estimated incentives.
3. Confirm tax-credit eligibility with a qualified tax professional before relying on it.
4. Ask whether any incentive requires pre-approval before installation begins.
5. Track who submits each form: homeowner, installer, lender, utility, or tax professional.

## Questions To Ask

- Which incentives are included in this quote, and who submits the paperwork?
- Are any incentives capped, waitlisted, income-qualified, or time-limited?
- What happens to the project economics if an incentive is reduced or unavailable?
- Is the quoted monthly payment calculated before or after assuming I apply an incentive?
- Does the contract require me to send an incentive amount to the lender later?
- Which incentive assumptions are written into the proposal and which are only verbal?

## What Good Looks Like

<div class="homeowner-checklist">
  <label><input type="checkbox" /> The quote shows gross project cost before incentives.</label>
  <label><input type="checkbox" /> Each estimated incentive is listed separately with timing, responsible party, and eligibility caveats.</label>
  <label><input type="checkbox" /> The homeowner has confirmed tax-related claims with a qualified tax professional.</label>
  <label><input type="checkbox" /> The contract explains what happens if an estimated incentive changes or is unavailable.</label>

## Watch-Outs

- Treating tax-related estimates as personalized tax advice.
- Comparing a net price from one quote against a gross price from another.
- Missing expiration dates, funding caps, or documentation requirements.
- Financing a project based on an incentive amount that may arrive later or not at all.
- Assuming the installer is responsible for every form unless the contract says so.
