## What You Will Learn

- Understand the difference between solar-only and backup-capable systems.
- Define critical loads before comparing battery sizes.
- Set clear expectations for outage duration and cost.
- Decide when a battery is essential, optional, or not worth the added complexity.

## Real-World Scenarios

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Scenario 1: the homeowner wants the whole house running during a long outage.</strong>
  <p>A single battery may not support central air, electric heat, ovens, well pumps, and other large loads at the same time. List critical circuits first, then design backup around those loads.</p>
</div>

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Scenario 2: outages are short, but evening rates are high.</strong>
  <p>The battery may serve bill management instead of emergency backup. Ask whether the rate plan, export credit, and battery controls support that use case.</p>
</div>

<div class="field-note">
  <strong>Scenario 3: the quote says "battery ready."</strong>
  <p>This may only mean a battery can be added later. It may not include backup wiring, transfer equipment, a critical-loads panel, permits, or battery hardware.</p>
</div>

## Key Ideas

### Batteries Are Designed Around Loads

A backup plan starts with the circuits or appliances that matter most, such as refrigeration, internet, medical devices, lighting, or limited heating and cooling.

### Runtime Depends on Both Capacity and Behavior

Battery capacity, appliance power draw, weather, solar recharge, and household usage determine how long backup power lasts.

### Backup Adds Design and Permitting Details

Backup panels, transfer equipment, control settings, utility requirements, and inspection steps can add cost and schedule complexity.

## Practical Steps

1. List the circuits or appliances you want available during an outage.
2. Estimate how often outages happen and how long they usually last.
3. Ask whether battery controls, permits, and utility rules affect your project timeline.
4. Separate must-have loads from nice-to-have loads.
5. Ask whether the battery can recharge from solar during an outage and what conditions are required.

## Questions To Ask

- Which loads will be backed up, and which will not?
- How much usable battery capacity is included?
- Can the battery recharge from solar during an extended outage?
- What is the maximum continuous and surge power the battery can provide?
- Is a critical-loads panel, transfer switch, or extra electrical work included?
- What settings control when the battery saves energy for backup versus daily bill management?

## Watch-Outs

- Expecting a battery to power the whole home without checking load limits.
- Comparing battery prices without usable capacity and backed-up circuits.
- Forgetting that large appliances can drain backup capacity quickly.
- Assuming "battery ready" means backup power is already included.
- Ignoring battery placement, temperature limits, fire code clearances, or internet requirements.
